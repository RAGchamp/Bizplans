Executive Summary

Agentic AI companies — LangChain, CrewAI, Sourcegraph, and dozens of newer startups — all face the same expensive problem: generating qualified enterprise leads is slow and costly. Outbound sales, conference sponsorships, and paid search all cost real money per lead, and most of that spend is wasted on unqualified traffic. Meanwhile, the buyer side of the market — Principal Engineers and Senior/Enterprise Architects asked to "explore agentic AI" for a specific business problem — has no single place to describe that problem in plain English and see which vendors actually solve it.

The Agentic AI Marketplace sits in between. A Principal Engineer or Senior Architect types their business problem into a search box. A retrieval-augmented (RAG-style) matching engine compares that problem against a structured catalogue of Agentic AI vendor solutions and returns a ranked shortlist of genuine fits — not a generic search-engine results page. If the user wants to go further, they fill out a short intake form, and their details are routed directly to the matching vendor(s).

This plan uses one revenue mechanism, applied at scale — lead-generation arbitrage. The base case is simple: if a vendor is willing to pay $10 for a lead, and the Marketplace can generate that lead for $5, the Marketplace keeps the $5 spread. That $10/$5 pairing is just the entry-level tier, though — more qualified leads command a higher vendor price without a proportionally higher generation cost, which lifts the margin well above $5/lead for those tiers (see Lead Pricing & Packaging). Blended across the full tier mix, this plan targets:

~20,000 monetized leads in Year 1, at a blended arbitrage margin of ~$14.75/lead (the weighted average across all three tiers — not the flat $5 the base case alone would suggest), generating ~$295,000 in gross arbitrage margin.

Against an estimated ~$8,600 in Year-1 fixed operating costs (lead-generation costs are already netted into the margin above), this lands at an estimated ~$286,400 pre-tax profit — and roughly ~$200,500 after an assumed ~30% tax rate — clearing a $175K after-tax income goal with a real margin of safety (see Net Profit Model), which matters because this two-sided marketplace model is inherently less proven than a single-sided subscription product.

2. The Problem

  • Agentic AI vendors (LangChain, CrewAI, Sourcegraph, and many earlier-stage startups) all need a steady flow of enterprise leads, but generating them is expensive: outbound SDR headcount, conference sponsorships, and paid search all carry a high cost-per-qualified-lead, and much of that spend is wasted on people who were never a real fit.
  • The buyer side — a Principal Engineer or Senior/Enterprise Architect told to "find an agentic AI solution for X" — faces the mirror-image problem: dozens of vendors, inconsistent marketing claims, and no neutral place to describe the actual business problem and get matched to a real fit. Today they're stuck Googling, reading vendor blogs, or cold-emailing sales teams who may or may not be relevant.
  • There is no neutral, problem-first marketplace that lets a technical buyer describe their problem in plain language and get matched against a real catalogue of agentic AI solutions — the way there is for dev tools broadly (G2, Capterra), but purpose-built for the agentic AI category specifically.
  • This gap is the business opportunity: build the matching layer once, and monetize it by selling the resulting leads to the vendors who would otherwise have to buy that same attention through expensive outbound or ads.

This is a two-sided problem — vendors need cheaper leads, buyers need a neutral matching layer — and the Marketplace is the mechanism that solves both at once by sitting in the middle.

3. The Product

What it is

A problem-first Agentic AI Marketplace: a Principal Engineer or Senior Architect describes a business problem in natural language; the platform returns a ranked shortlist of matching Agentic AI solutions from a structured vendor catalogue; the user can click through to a vendor or submit a short intake form for a more qualified match.

Example catalogue entries (illustrative):

Vendor / CategoryExample business problem it matches
LangChain / LangGraph — agent orchestration framework"We need to orchestrate a multi-step agent workflow with human-in-the-loop approval checkpoints."
CrewAI — multi-agent collaboration framework"We need several specialized agents to collaborate autonomously on a complex task without a human coordinating each step."
Sourcegraph (Cody/Amp) — code-intelligence agent tooling"We need an AI agent that understands our entire codebase to help with a large-scale refactor or migration."
(additional vertical agent startups, added over time)Customer-support triage agents, sales/RevOps qualification agents, compliance-review agents, etc.
User journey
  1. A Principal Engineer / Senior Architect types their business problem into the Marketplace search box (e.g., "We want to automate tier-1 customer support tickets with an AI agent that can escalate to a human when confidence is low.").
  2. A retrieval-augmented (RAG-style) matching engine compares the problem statement against a structured catalogue of vendor solutions and returns a ranked shortlist of genuine fits — ranked by matching relevance, not by who pays the most.
  3. Tier 1 — Click-through: the user clicks "View Solution" and is redirected to the vendor's site/trial signup, tracked via a referral link.
  4. Tier 2 — Qualified lead: the user fills a short intake form (company, role, timeline, budget band) attached to their problem statement, routed directly to the vendor's sales team.
  5. Tier 3 — Sales-qualified warm intro: a platform concierge confirms fit, budget, and timeline, then books an intro call directly on the vendor's account executive's calendar.

The vendor is billed per delivered lead, at whichever tier — see Lead Pricing & Packaging

Why "neutral matching" is the product, not just search

Unlike a vendor's own marketing site or a generic search engine, the Marketplace's value proposition to the buyer is that the shortlist is genuinely matched to their stated problem and ranked by fit — not sponsored placement. That neutrality is what earns repeat use and word-of-mouth from the buyer side, which in turn is what makes the leads valuable enough for vendors to pay for.

4. Target Market — A Two-Sided Marketplace

This is a two-sided marketplace, which means there are two distinct customers to acquire and satisfy.

Side A — Buyers (the searchers, who never pay)
Principal Engineers, Senior/Enterprise Architects, and similar technical decision-makers at mid-to-large enterprises asked to find or evaluate an agentic AI solution for a specific business problem. Far more active on LinkedIn and enterprise-architecture communities than the developer-forum/Discord audience of a typical dev-tool launch.
Side B — Payers (the vendors)
Agentic AI companies of all sizes — from well-known platforms (LangChain, CrewAI, Sourcegraph) to earlier-stage vertical-agent startups — who need cheaper, more qualified enterprise leads than outbound sales or paid ads currently deliver.

The chicken-and-egg problem: no vendors in the catalogue means no reason for buyers to search; no buyer traffic means no reason for vendors to pay. See Building the Vendor Catalogue for how this is bootstrapped before either side fully commits.

5. Business Model — Lead-Generation Arbitrage

The entire business model is one mechanism, applied at scale:

If a vendor will pay $10 for a lead, and the Marketplace can generate that lead for $5, the Marketplace keeps the $5 spread.

This is lead-generation arbitrage: the Marketplace takes on the cost and effort of matching, qualifying, and delivering a lead, and is paid by the vendor only for the leads it actually delivers (pay-per-performance, not pay-for-placement). There is no subscription fee charged to buyers — the entire revenue stream comes from vendors paying for delivered leads, at pricing tiered by qualification depth (see Lead Pricing & Packaging).

Note on the $10/$5 example: that pairing is only the Tier 1 (lowest-touch) case — it is not the number to multiply by 20,000 leads to check the Year-1 target, since the $5 margin only applies to Tier 1 leads. Tiers 2 and 3 carry higher vendor prices ($35 and $150) against generation costs that rise much less steeply ($12 and $40), producing much larger per-lead margins ($23 and $110). The ~$14.75/lead figure used elsewhere in this plan is the volume-weighted average across all three tiers, not the $5 base case scaled up.

Not all leads are worth the same to a vendor — a casual click-through is worth far less than a warm, sales-qualified intro. The Marketplace prices three lead tiers accordingly:

Blended mix assumption (Year 1): most searches are casual and stay low-touch, so the mix skews heavily toward Tier 1 — 70% Tier 1 / 25% Tier 2 / 5% Tier 3. This produces a blended average of ~$23.25 revenue and ~$14.75 margin per monetized lead.

There is no fee charged to the buyer, and no vendor listing fee in Year 1 — pure pay-per-delivered-lead keeps the pitch to vendors simple and removes their biggest objection to trying a new channel: zero upfront cost, pay only for what you get.

7. Path to $175K/Year — The Arbitrage Funnel

The entire financial plan is one formula:

Goal: 20,000 monetized leads × ~$14.75 blended margin ≈ $295,000 gross arbitrage margin — clears the $175K after-tax income target with a real margin of safety.

Assumption: a 40% search-to-lead conversion rate — much higher than a typical subscription funnel, because clicking through or filling a form costs the buyer nothing but time. Implied qualified searches needed: 20,000 ÷ 40% = 50,000 qualified marketplace searches/year (~965/week).

8. Building the Vendor Catalogue — The Supply Side

Before there's meaningful buyer traffic, there's no reason for a vendor to sign a paid per-lead agreement — so the catalogue has to be bootstrapped with low/no-risk pilots first.

Steps
  1. Build a target list of 30–50 Agentic AI vendors across categories — orchestration frameworks (LangChain/LangGraph, CrewAI), code-intelligence agents (Sourcegraph), and vertical agent startups (support, sales/RevOps, compliance) — sourced from Product Hunt launches, YC batches, G2 "AI agent" categories, and LangChain/CrewAI partner ecosystems.
  2. Seed the catalogue editorially first, using public information about each vendor's capabilities, before any formal paid deal exists — this makes search results genuinely useful from day one, independent of which vendors have signed on.
  3. Pitch the first 2–3 anchor vendors on a zero-upfront-cost pilot: "we'll send you pre-qualified leads; you only pay for the ones you accept as qualified — no cost if it doesn't work." This is an easy yes for a vendor's growth/partnerships team since there's no downside.
  4. Define a written SLA per tier (what counts as a "qualified" lead) before the first invoice, to avoid payment disputes later — this is the single most common failure point in lead-gen arbitrage businesses.
  5. Set up lead routing — a simple webhook/email integration is enough for early pilot vendors; move to CRM-to-CRM integration (Salesforce/HubSpot) once a vendor relationship scales.
  6. Turn the first pilot results into a case study ("we sent Vendor X 40 qualified leads last quarter at $35/lead, a 66% margin for us and a fraction of their blended CAC") and use it to recruit the next wave of vendors.
  7. Diversify deliberately — target 10–15 vendors onboarded by year-end so no single vendor relationship represents a majority of revenue.
  8. Encourage vendor co-marketing — once a vendor sees real qualified leads coming in, ask them to link back to their Marketplace catalogue listing from their own site/docs. This is a demand-generation channel unique to a two-sided marketplace: the vendors themselves help drive buyer traffic, because it's free distribution for them too.
Target by end of Year 1

10–15 onboarded vendors, collectively responsible for the full $465,000 in vendor lead-payment revenue (averaging roughly $31,000–$46,500 in revenue per vendor across the year).

12. Go-to-Market Plan

Phase 1 — Bootstrap Both Sides (Mo 1–3)
  • Build the MVP: problem-search UI, a RAG-based matching engine, and a catalogue seeded editorially with 5–8 vendor listings before any paid deal exists
  • Sign 2–3 anchor vendors to a zero-upfront-cost pilot (pay only for accepted leads)
  • Launch publicly (Product Hunt, Hacker News, LinkedIn) to prove the matching engine produces leads vendors actually accept and pay for
Phase 2 — Scale Both Sides Together (Mo 4–8)
  • Expand the vendor catalogue to 8–10 vendors across more categories; formalize per-lead SLAs and invoicing
  • Double down on whichever demand channel produces the cheapest qualified searches
  • Introduce the Tier 3 concierge/warm-intro offering once there's enough volume to justify the higher-touch work
Phase 3 — Optimize the Flywheel (Mo 9–12)
  • Use real conversion data to improve matching-engine ranking quality
  • Push vendors to cross-link their catalogue listing, compounding organic buyer traffic
  • Begin exploring a contracted monthly lead-volume retainer with top vendors for Year 2

Core GTM insight: unlike a single-sided product, growth here is a flywheel — better vendor catalogue → better buyer matches → more buyer trust and traffic → more valuable leads → easier to recruit the next vendor. Both sides have to be actively worked in parallel from day one.

13. Strategic Advantage / Moat

  • Two-sided network effects — a bigger, better-matched vendor catalogue makes the Marketplace more useful to buyers, which generates more (and more valuable) leads, which makes it easier to recruit the next vendor. This flywheel is hard for a single vendor to replicate on its own.
  • Neutral matching is more trustworthy than vendor marketing — a buyer trusts an independent, problem-first shortlist more than any single vendor's sales pitch, especially early in evaluation — this is the core reason buyers keep coming back.
  • Ranking integrity as a differentiator — matching relevance (RAG relevance score) determines the default ranking; any paid boosting is clearly labeled "Sponsored," similar to how G2/Capterra protect buyer trust. This is a deliberate constraint, not just an ethical stance — it's what keeps the leads valuable enough for vendors to keep paying for them.
  • Zero-upfront-cost, pay-per-performance pricing removes the vendor's biggest objection — trying a new lead channel carries no downside risk for them, which is the easiest possible pitch to get the first anchor vendors on board.
  • Strong unit economics once both sides are working — a ~63% blended arbitrage margin means profitability doesn't depend on heavy fixed costs or a large team; the model scales through the mix of lead tiers, not through outspending competitors on ads.

16. Summary

The plan rests on a single mechanism: a problem-first Agentic AI Marketplace that matches a buyer's business problem to a vendor's solution, and monetizes the resulting lead through tiered pay-per-performance arbitrage — vendors pay $10–$150 per lead depending on qualification depth, at a cost to generate of $5–$40, for a blended margin of ~$14.75/lead. Hitting the goal of 20,000 monetized leads (≈$295,000 gross arbitrage margin) requires roughly 50,000 qualified searches at a 40% search-to-lead conversion rate, built on top of a vendor catalogue bootstrapped through zero-upfront-cost pilots with anchor vendors like LangChain, CrewAI, and Sourcegraph. Against ~$8,600 in Year-1 fixed operating costs, this plan lands at an estimated ~$286,400 pre-tax profit — and roughly ~$200,500 after an assumed ~30% tax rate — clearing the $175K after-tax income goal with meaningful margin of safety, reflecting the fact that a two-sided marketplace carries more unproven variables than a single-sided subscription product.
