Executive Summary

Enterprise software sales professionals selling Agentic AI — Account Executives, Sales Engineers, Solutions Consultants, and booth staff — routinely walk into rooms with skeptical senior technical buyers (CTOs, platform architects, enterprise AI leads) and pitch "autonomous agents" in language indistinguishable from every other vendor that buyer met that week. Based on direct, first-hand observation of these pitches, the recurring failure is not product quality — it's that reps don't know where their own product sits in the Agentic AI ecosystem, so they can't match their pitch to what that specific buyer actually cares about.

Agentic AI Positioning Training closes that gap with one memorable diagnostic question: "Are you selling cars, car parts, an Uber ride, or a rental car?" All four deal in cars; all four require a completely different pitch. Mapped onto Agentic AI — foundation models (cars), infrastructure/tooling (car parts), managed autonomous agents (Uber), and enterprise agent-building platforms (rental) — the framework gives a rep a fast way to diagnose their lane, then a matching set of buyer personas, discovery questions, and objection responses for that lane.

This plan deliberately runs on a simple, three-tier revenue model so a solo operator can build and run it without a services team:

A free diagnostic tier for top-of-funnel reach, a $395/year Pro subscription for the full curriculum, and a $795 one-time Booth/Conference Specialist track for reps staffing high-traffic Agentic AI conferences (events at venues like the Javits Center). Year-1 target: ~5,000 free signups → 400 Pro subscribers (8% conversion) plus 150 Booth-track seats sold across three conference cycles ≈ $277,250 in total Year-1 revenue.

Against an estimated $39,750 in total Year-1 operating costs (content production, platform/community hosting, marketing, conference presence, payment processing, and basic legal/LLC setup), this lands at an estimated ~$237,500 pre-tax profit (≈86% margin) — and roughly ~$166,250 after an assumed ~30% tax rate (see Net Profit Model).

2. The Problem

  • No ecosystem map. Reps don't know if they're pitching infrastructure, an application, a platform, or a fully-managed outcome — so they describe capabilities ("our agents can reason, plan, and act autonomously") instead of position (what layer of the stack they occupy, and who else is in the room).
  • No buyer-to-layer mapping. The same deck gets pitched to a platform engineering lead (who cares about extensibility, control, data governance) and a business-unit VP (who cares about time-to-value and headcount leverage) — using identical language.
  • No problem anchor. Senior technical buyers ask "what breaks in my environment today that this fixes?" and reps answer with product features, not organizational pain (integration debt, shadow IT, compliance exposure, headcount cost, tool sprawl, orchestration complexity).
  • Conference booth chaos. At high-volume events, booth staff get ~90 seconds per visitor and no repeatable framework to triage a stranger into "not a fit," "nurture," or "hot — bring in an SE now." Most booths run on charisma and caffeine, not a system.
  • There is currently no training product built specifically for this. Generic sales-methodology courses and free "what is an AI agent" explainer content both exist in abundance — neither teaches ecosystem positioning for a skeptical enterprise technical buyer. That gap is the business opportunity.

4. Target Market

Primary buyer/user (Pro tier): Account Executives, Enterprise AEs, Sales Engineers, and Solutions Consultants at Agentic AI / AI-infrastructure software companies selling into large enterprise IT organizations. Secondary: SDR/BDR leads who need to qualify before AEs get involved, and Sales Enablement/RevOps leaders buying seats for a team.

Secondary buyer (Booth tier): reps and SEs personally staffing booths at high-traffic Agentic AI conferences, plus Field Marketing managers responsible for booth performance, and founders/early sales hires at seed/Series A companies who work their own booth.

1. Growth-stage Agentic AI vendors
50–300+ quota-carrying reps; likely to buy team/enterprise Pro licenses through Sales Enablement.
2. Early-stage / seed–Series A vendors
3–15 reps, thin or no internal enablement function — the clearest individual-subscriber wedge.
3. Conference-exhibiting vendors of any size
Send 2–6 booth staff per major event — the Booth-track buyer, refreshed every event cycle.

This is a deliberately narrow, high-willingness-to-pay niche, not a horizontal SaaS play (see Strategic Advantage / Moat) — the cost of a botched enterprise pitch, or a wasted conference badge scan, is expensive and visible to the buyer's employer, which is what supports the price point at this market size.

13. Strategic Advantage / Moat

  • Price removes the approval blocker. $395/year is self-expensable by an individual rep or a single manager sign-off; $795 is easily covered by an existing event/booth budget line rather than a training line — both bypass the procurement friction that kills most B2B training sales.
  • The framework itself is the differentiator. "Cars, car parts, Uber, or rental" is simple and memorable enough to spread organically on LinkedIn, yet has enough depth (buyer mapping, objection banks, live cohorts) to justify payment — a rare combination.
  • Seasonal, recurring purchase trigger. The Booth track is tied to the conference calendar, creating a natural repeat-purchase cycle that a generic evergreen course doesn't have.
  • Practitioner credibility is hard to copy. The content is built on direct, first-hand pattern recognition from real enterprise pitches — "I've sat across the table and watched this fail" is not something a generic sales-training competitor can replicate quickly.
  • Strong unit economics once distribution works. An implied ~$19.83 blended cost per paying Pro subscriber against $395 in first-year revenue (and more in renewal years) means profitability doesn't depend on outspending anyone on ads.

16. Summary

The plan rests on one memorable diagnostic — "are you selling cars, car parts, an Uber ride, or a rental car?" — taught free, then monetized through a $395/year Pro subscription and a $795 Booth/Conference Specialist track tied to the live conference calendar. Hitting the Year-1 goal of 400 Pro subscribers and 150 Booth seats (≈$277,250 revenue) requires roughly 5,000 free signups at an 8% conversion rate, sourced mostly from organic LinkedIn content, conference-floor presence, and practitioner referrals rather than paid ads. Against ~$39,750 in total Year-1 operating costs, this plan lands at an estimated ~$237,500 pre-tax profit — and roughly ~$166,250 after an assumed ~30% tax rate — a lean, high-margin niche business with team/enterprise licensing as the clear Year-2 scale lever.
